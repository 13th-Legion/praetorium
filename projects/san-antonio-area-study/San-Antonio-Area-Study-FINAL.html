Key Findings

Threat Environment

The San Antonio metro area presents a moderate threat environment for conservative political organizations. The region has a history of civil unrest, particularly related to police accountability, immigration, and social justice issues, amplified by its proximity to military installations and the U.S.-Mexico border.

Primary Threat Actors: - DSA San Antonio — Local chapter of Democratic Socialists of America; focused on labor, housing, and electoral organizing with potential for protest mobilization - Black Lives Matter affiliates — Informal networks active in police oversight campaigns; led major 2020 demonstrations - John Brown Gun Club elements — Armed left-wing presence documented at 2022-2023 events; overlaps with broader activist networks - Immigration/anti-ICE activists — Heightened activity due to border proximity; coordinated with national networks like 50501

Secondary Considerations: - Military base proximity (JBSA installations) may complicate event planning and create unique security dynamics - SAPD oversight movements (e.g., Fix SAPD) maintain pressure on law enforcement, potentially spilling over to conservative events

Geographic Layout

The San Antonio-New Braunfels MSA spans approximately 7,387 square miles across 8 counties. Key geographic features relevant to security planning:

  • Urban Core: Downtown San Antonio, River Walk, Alamo historic district
  • Military Installations: JBSA-Fort Sam Houston (northeast), JBSA-Lackland (southwest), JBSA-Randolph (northeast)
  • Suburban Ring: New Braunfels (northeast), Boerne (northwest), areas along I-35 corridor
  • Border Proximity: ~150 miles from U.S.-Mexico border; influences immigration-related activism

Transportation

  • Highways: I-35 (north-south), I-10 (east-west), I-37 (to Corpus Christi), Loop 410, Loop 1604
  • Airports: San Antonio International (SAT), Stinson Municipal
  • Transit: VIA Metropolitan Transit (bus system), limited light rail

Political Landscape

Overview

San Antonio is politically diverse, with the urban core leaning Democratic and suburbs more conservative. Bexar County has trended Democratic in recent presidential elections, but state-level politics remain Republican-dominated.

Key Political Dynamics

  1. Urban-Suburban Divide: Conservative groups may find more support in suburban and exurban areas, while downtown events face higher protest risk.
  2. Military Influence: Large active-duty and veteran population; generally conservative-leaning but with diverse views.
  3. Border Issues: Proximity to Mexico heightens immigration debates; local activism often focuses on ICE operations and border policies.
  4. Police Accountability: Ongoing movements for SAPD reform influence local politics.

4.1 Timeline of Key Incidents

2020: George Floyd Protests

May 30, 2020 - Downtown Protest Escalation
- Location: Travis Park to Alamo Plaza
- Description: Initially peaceful rally of ~1,000+ protesting George Floyd’s death. Escalated after dark with vandalism, fires, and clashes.
- Police Response: SAPD deployed tear gas, pepper spray, and kinetic impact projectiles (sponge rounds). Multiple protesters injured, including eye injuries.
- Arrests: ~50+
- Relevance: Demonstrated potential for rapid escalation; led to lawsuits and ongoing police reform demands. Armed militia (This Is Texas Freedom Force) present behind police lines.
- Sources: KSAT, Express-News, TPR

June 1-7, 2020 - Sustained Demonstrations
- Locations: Various downtown sites, City Hall
- Description: Daily protests with hundreds to thousands participating; focused on local police accountability.
- Notable: Peaceful marches, speeches; some property damage.
- Relevance: Built momentum for SAPD oversight movements like Fix SAPD.

2021-2022: Post-Dobbs and Single-Issue Protests

June 2022 - Abortion Rights Demonstrations
- Location: Downtown, Planned Parenthood sites
- Description: Protests following Dobbs decision; hundreds rallied against Texas abortion laws.
- Relevance: Coalition building between DSA, BLM, and feminist groups. Low violence.

December 2022 - Drag Show Confrontations
- Location: St. Mary’s Strip venue
- Description: Right-wing protesters confronted by armed counter-protesters, including JBGC members.
- Relevance: Showed armed left-wing presence; tensions but no major violence.

2023-2024: Police Reform and Immigration Focus

August 2023 - Police Contract Protests
- Location: City Hall
- Description: Activists protested new SAPD union contract, demanding more oversight.
- Relevance: Fix SAPD-led; highlighted accountability issues. Council approved contract 8-3 despite opposition.

Various 2024 - Border-Related Demonstrations
- Locations: Near JBSA sites, downtown
- Description: Small-scale protests against state immigration policies and military involvement.
- Relevance: Building tension leading into 2025 escalations.

2025: Immigration Crackdown Backlash

June 11-14, 2025 - “No Kings” Protests
- Location: Alamo Plaza, downtown streets
- Description: Thousands protested Trump administration policies, including mass deportations. Part of national 50501 movement. Peaceful but large-scale; chants against ICE and border militarization.
- Police/Military Response: National Guard deployed at Alamo; SAPD monitored. No major clashes.
- Relevance: Largest recent mobilization; highlighted anti-ICE sentiment near border.

November 17, 2025 - Anti-Raid Protest
- Location: San Pedro Ave & Basse Rd
- Description: ~100+ protested federal raid detaining 150+ suspected of gang ties. Focused on immigration enforcement tactics.
- Relevance: Direct response to local ICE/DPS operation; potential for escalation in border-proximate area.

4.2 Patterns and Analysis

  • Escalation Triggers: Police use of force (2020), federal immigration actions (2025), cultural events (2022).
  • Key Locations: Alamo Plaza and downtown repeatedly serve as focal points.
  • Group Involvement: BLM-led in 2020; broader coalitions including DSA and immigration groups in 2025.
  • Military Proximity: Protests near JBSA gates could involve federal jurisdiction.
  • Trend: Shift from racial justice to immigration focus, correlated with national politics.

5.1 Protest & Direct Action Tactics

Standard Protest Tactics

Marches & Rallies: - Permitted or spontaneous marches in downtown areas - Rallies at symbolic sites (Alamo Plaza, City Hall, La Villita) - Signs, chants, megaphones - Protected under First Amendment

Civil Disobedience: - Street blockades - Sit-ins at government buildings or raid sites - Defiance of dispersal orders - Aimed at media coverage and disruption

Noise Demonstrations: - Drums, horns, pots/pans outside targets - Used to harass or disrupt events

Escalated Tactics

Property Destruction: - Vandalism, graffiti - Window smashing (rare in SA) - Arson attempts (2020 protests) - Focused on symbols of authority

Direct Confrontation: - Blocking access to venues - Mobbing individuals - Pushing/shoving - Improvised weapons

Armed Presence: - Legal open carry by JBGC at events - Defensive positioning - Pepper spray use documented
